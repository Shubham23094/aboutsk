About Me

I am interested in developing machine learning systems that can support scientific discovery. My current work explores how language models can generate research ideas, how reward models can estimate scientific impact, and how reinforcement learning can be used to optimize idea generation while preserving feasibility and mechanistic clarity.

A central question in my research is whether AI systems can move beyond producing plausible-sounding ideas and instead generate proposals that are technically grounded, novel, testable, and likely to be impactful. I am especially interested in preventing reward hacking, shallow novelty, and infeasible idea generation in impact-oriented optimization settings.

Research Interests

My work sits at the intersection of language modeling, scientific forecasting, reinforcement learning, and AI-assisted research.

Impact-Aligned Idea Generation

Training language models to generate research proposals optimized for predicted scientific impact while maintaining feasibility, clarity, and concrete technical mechanisms.

Reward Modeling

Designing ordinal and preference-style reward models that estimate the likely impact of research ideas from goal-conditioned proposal descriptions.

RL for Scientific Discovery

Studying how reinforcement learning can improve scientific idea generation without encouraging superficial novelty, citation bias, or reward hacking.

Scientific Forecasting

Predicting future research impact using citation-normalized signals, temporal splits, field normalization, and robust evaluation protocols.

Research Understanding

Extracting paper-agnostic research goals and mechanistic idea descriptions from scientific papers for downstream modeling and evaluation.

Trustworthy Generation

Building constraints and evaluation methods that reduce hallucination, infeasible methods, vague proposals, and over-optimized reward artifacts.
